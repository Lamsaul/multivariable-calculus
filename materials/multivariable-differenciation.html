Derivatives of Multivariable Functions

The Idea

  • Multivariable functions take \( n \) independent variables to return the value of one dependent variable.
  • While a function \( f(x) \) may be a line or more generally a curve in 2-D, a function \( f(x,y) \) will extend to 3-D as a surface.
  • The derivative of this function at some point in this case wouldn't simply be the gradient of the tangent line to the surface as it'd be too ambiguous due to the number of tangent lines that can be drawn against the surface, all with varying directions.
  • To find the gradient of the tangent line to a surface at a point, we have to give it a direction.
  • This direction can, in the most simple cases, be the \( \hat{i} \) or \( \hat{j} \) in 3 dimensions, and can be extended along any unit vector in the plane of the independent variables as a general directional derivative.

Directional Derivatives along \(\hat{i}\) and \(\hat{j}\)

  • When we calculate the gradient along the \( x \) direction (along \( \hat{i} \)), we'd have to contain the tangent line in a plane parallel to the \( xz \) plane.
  • Since this only contains the \( x \) axis, we will require \( y \) to be a constant.
  • And similarly, when we calculate the gradient along the \( y \) direction, we require \( x \) to be a constant.
  • This idea of treating a variable as a constant and thereby ensuring that the tangent line is in the direction of the other variable is called a partial derivative.
  • Notation for a partial derivative with respect to \( x \): \[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \text{ or } f_x \]

Directional Derivatives along \(\hat{u}\)

  • If we were to extend the idea for calculating the derivative only along the axis of one independent variable, we'd encounter directional derivatives that can be calculated along any direction \(\hat{u}=u_1 \hat{i} + u_2 \hat{j}\) where \(\hat{u}\) is a unit vector on the plane of independent variables.
  • This directional derivative is denoted, for some function \(f(x,y)\) and vector \(\hat{u}\) as \(D_{\hat{u}} f(x,y)\) and is calculated as:
    • \[D_{\hat{u}} f(x,y) = f_x u_1 + f_y u_2\]
  • This expression can be rewritten as a dot product, which gives us the vector \( \hat{u} \) on one side and a 'weird' vector containing the derivatives with respect to each independent variable as components to the axis unit vectors of those respective independent variables: \[ D_{\hat{u}} f(x,y) = (f_x \hat{i} + f_y \hat{j}) \cdot (u_1 \hat{i} + u_2 \hat{j}) \]
  • This 'weird' vector is called the gradient vector and is denoted as \( \nabla f \). The expression for \( D_{\hat{u}} f(x,y) \) can hence be rewritten as: \[ D_{\hat{u}} f(x,y) = \nabla f \cdot \hat{u} \]

Tangent Lines and Planes

  • \( \nabla f \) is the normal to the level curve of \( f(x,y) = c \).
  • \( \nabla f \) is the normal to the level surface of \( f(x,y,z) = d \).
  • Tangent lines and planes both need the normal vector.

Example: Determining tangent and normal lines (2-D)

  • As an example, take \( x^2 - y^2 = 16 \). To find the tangent line and normal line, we can 'cheat' by pretending that this is a level curve to some surface in \( \mathbb{R}^3 \).
  • Doing so, we get something like: \( f(x,y) = x^2 - y^2 \) (assuming that 16 was some value of \( c \) in \( f(x,y) = c \)).
  • This would give us \( \nabla f(x,y) = 2x \hat{i} - 2y \hat{j} \), which can be interpreted as the normal vector to any level curve \( x^2 - y^2 = c \).
  • An example point \( P(5,3) \) gives a *specific normal* to the level curve \( x^2 - y^2 = 16 \). To determine that normal, we first need to determine \( \nabla f(5,3) \): \[ \nabla f(5,3) = 10 \hat{i} - 6 \hat{j} \]
  • The gradient of the normal line can hence be determined as: \[ m_N = \frac{-6}{10} = \frac{-3}{5} \]
  • The equation can be obtained easily as: \[ y - 3 = -\frac{3}{5}(x - 5) \Rightarrow y = -\frac{3}{5}x + 6 \]
  • As the tangent is perpendicular to the normal, its gradient should be \( \frac{5}{3} \), letting us obtain the equation of the tangent as: \[ y - 3 = \frac{5}{3}(x - 5) \Rightarrow y = \frac{5}{3}x - \frac{16}{3} \]

Example: Determining tangent planes (3-D)

  • Take \( -x^2 + y^2 - z^2 = 4 \) and a point \( P(1,3,2) \) on it. Similar to the previous example, we can treat this as a level surface in \( \mathbb{R}^4 \).
  • Doing so, we again get something like: \( f(x,y,z) = -x^2 + y^2 - z^2 \), an equation of 4 dimensions.
  • \( f(x,y,z) = 4 \) is the level surface (out of a family of level surfaces) that contains the point \( P(1,3,2) \).
  • We can obtain the normal vector to this particular level surface at \( P(1,3,2) \) by calculating \( \nabla f(1,3,2) \): \[ \nabla f = -2 \hat{i} + 6 \hat{j} - 4 \hat{k} \]
  • The equation of the tangent plane can hence be obtained using the general Cartesian equation of a plane: \[ a(x - x_0) + b(y - y_0) + c(z - z_0) = 0 \]
  • Here, \( a,b,c \) represent the components of \( \hat{i}, \hat{j}, \) and \( \hat{k} \) respectively of the vector that is normal to the plane. So, the equation of the tangent plane is: \[ -2(x - 1) + 6(y - 3) - 4(z - 2) = 0 \] \[ -2x + 2 + 6y - 18 - 4z + 8 = 0 \Rightarrow -2x + 6y - 4z = 8 \]
