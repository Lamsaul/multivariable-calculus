Surface Integrals

\[ \int_S\int f(x,y,z)\cdot dS = \int_R\int f(x,y, g(x,y)) \sqrt{g_x^2 +g_y^2+1} \cdot dA \] \[ \int_S \int f(x, y, z) \cdot dS = \int_R \int f(x,g(x,z),z) \sqrt{g_x^2 + g_z^2+1} \cdot dA \] \[ \int_S \int f(x, y, z) \cdot dS = \int_R \int f(g(y,z),y,z) \sqrt{g_y^2 + g_z^2+1} \cdot dA \]

Example

\[ \int_S \int x+2y+z \cdot dS \]

For \(y+z=4\) inside \(x^2+y^2=1\).

Let \(z=g(x,y)\), so \(g(x,y)=4-y\).

Now, we get,

\[ \int_R \int x+2y+4-y \sqrt{0^2 + 1 + 1}\cdot dA=\sqrt{2}\int_R \int x+y+4\cdot dA \] \[ \sqrt{2}\int_0^{2\pi} \int_{r=0}^{r=1} r (r\cos{\theta} + r\sin{\theta}+4) \cdot dr \cdot d\theta=4\sqrt{2}\pi \]

Parametric Surfaces

  • For \(f(x,y,z)\) and \(x=x(u,v)\), \(y=y(u,v)\), and \(z=z(u,v)\), let \[ \vec{r}(u,v)=x(u,v)\hat{i} + y(u,v)\hat{j} + z(u,v)\hat{k} \]
  • Surface area is given by \[ \int_D \int ||r_u \times r_v|| \cdot dA \]
  • Where \(D\) is the domain of \(u\) and \(v\).
  • Parametric surface integral is given as \[ \int_D \int f(r(u,v))||r_u \times r_v|| \cdot dA \]

Flux

  • If \(F\), a vector field, contains a surface \(S\), then \(F\) describes the flow/fluid at any point across the surface.
  • The rate of flow is called the flux.
  • Flux is given by \[ \int_S\int \textbf{F} \cdot dS \]
  • For a normal vector at any point in the surface, \[ \int_S \int \textbf{F} \cdot dS=\int_S \int \textbf{F} \cdot \hat{n} \cdot dS \]
  • If the fluid has a mass density at a point \(\rho(x,y,z)\), the mass of the fluid flowing across \(S\) is given by \[ \int_S \int \rho \cdot \textbf{F} \cdot \hat{n} \cdot dS \]
  • For \(\textbf{F}=P\hat{i}+Q\hat{j}+M\hat{k}\), \[ \int_S\int \textbf{F} \cdot dS = \int_S\int \textbf{F} \cdot \hat{n} \cdot dS = \int_R \int (-Pg_x-Qg_y+M) \cdot dA \]
  • Where \(z=g(x,y)\).

The Divergence Theorem

\[ \text{Flux}=\int_S\int \textbf{F} \cdot dS = \int_T\int\int \text{Div}(F) \cdot dV \]

Example Problem

Find the flux through the vector field

\[ F(x,y,z)=(x+\sin{z})\hat{i} + (2y+\cos{x})\hat{j} + (3z+\tan{y})\hat{k} \]

over the surface \(x^2+y^2+z^2=4\).

\[ \text{Div}(F) = 6 \]

\[ \int_S\int \textbf{F} \cdot dS = \int_T \int \int 6 \cdot dV \]

We can integrate it in spherical coordinates. So, \(0 \leq \rho \leq 2\), \(0 \leq \phi \leq \pi\), \(0\leq \theta \leq 2\pi\),

\[ \int_{0}^{2\pi} \int_0^\pi \int_0^2 6\rho^2 \sin{\phi} \cdot d\rho d\phi d\theta = 64\pi \]

Stokes Theorem

\[ w = \oint_C \textbf{F} \cdot dr = \int_S \int \text{Curl}(\textbf{F}) \cdot dS \]

\(S\) is not a plane.
