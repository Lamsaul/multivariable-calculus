Problem 3

  1. In general, the moment of inertia around an axis (a line) \(L\) is, \[I_L = \int\int_R dist(\cdot, L)^2 \delta \cdot dA \] The collection of lines parallel to the y-axis have the form \(x = a\). Let \(I = I_y\) be the usual moment of inertia around the y-axis, \[I=\int\int_Rx^2\delta \cdot dA\] Let \(\bar{I}\) be the moment of inertia around the axis \(x=\bar{x}\), where \((\bar{x}, \bar{y})\) is the center of mass. Show that \[I = \bar{I} + M\bar{x}^2\]

Solution

\(x\) coordinate relative to \(\bar{x}\) is given as, \[x' = x-\bar{x} \Rightarrow x=x'+\bar{x}\] \[I = \int\int_R \delta(x'+\bar{x})^2 \cdot dA = \int\int_R \delta (x'^2 + 2x'\bar{x} + \bar{x}^2) \cdot dA\] \[I = \int\int_R \delta x'^2 \cdot dA + 2\bar{x}\int\int_R x'\delta \cdot dA + \int \int_R \delta \bar{x}^2 \cdot dA\] \[\int\int_R \delta x'^2 \cdot dA = \bar{I}\] \[\int\int_R x' \delta \cdot dA = 0\] As it is the first moment and the coordinate is already defined about the center of mass. \[\int \int_R \delta \bar{x}^2 \cdot dA= \bar{x}^2\int \int_R \delta \cdot dA = M\bar{x}^2\] \[\therefore I=\bar{I}+M\bar{x}^2\]
